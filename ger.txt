According to VisitScotland research Germany are Scotland's second biggest market. In 2012 Germans visited Scotland over 280 thousand times and spent over 140 million pounds. 

German visitor is typically wealthy and well-educated, with an interest in culture, environment and outdoor activities. The average age of German visitors to Scotland is slightly higher than European average. 

Most Germans, especially the younger ones, speak English quite well. When travelling to UK they appreciate the possibility to improve their language skills by interacting with locals.

Germans are the 'greenest' nation in Europe and nature has a big impact on their decision on holiday destination. They are also most willing to purchase local products and use public transport or other local services.

Germans relatively often come back to revisit Scotland. In 2012 36% of Germans visiting Scotland was there with repeat visit. The reason for that can be that Germans more often than other nations come to Scotland to visit friends or relatives.

Edinburgh City, the Highlands, Glasgow City and Perth and Perthshire are the most popular places in Scotland among the German visitors. On the average, Germans spend in Scotland seven days and spend daily over 70 pounds. Most visits fall on spring (35%) and summer (32%).

Holiday trips make up the vast majority of German visits to Scotland (63%). In recent years business become more and more important reason for German to go to Scotland - in 2012 business trips made up 18% of all visits.

The most important factors 
attracting Germans to Scotland are beautiful landscapes (76%) and history and culture (53%). Their favourite type of holiday is touring around a country by car that allows them to go off the beaten track and stop whenever they want. For younger Germans an opportunity to taste Scottish pub culture and experience the nightlife is very attractive.

The vast majority of German visitors get to Scotland by airplane. However, German tourists travelling to Scotland by sea or via the Channel Tunnel is also not unusual. 
Germans are used to a high standard of living. They expect accommodation of similar quality when on holidays and they are ready to pay more for a place that meet their requirements. A hotel is the first choice of most Germans, but B&B and guesthouses are also quite popular, especially since they offer the opportunity to interact with Scottish hosts, something that most German visitors desired.

Germans more often than other nations take into accounts opinions of other people. According to the VisitScotland survey nearly half of Germans have decided to try a new holiday destination, because it had been recommended by their friends or relatives.

---

The German tourist market has the huge potential. Germany are, after the United States, the world's second largest tourism market with over 85 million visits abroad every year.
In 2012, over 10% of all visitors to Scotland came from Germany. Their expenditures made up over 9% of all the money spent by visitors. In the next decade the further growth of the number of German visitors and their expenditures is expected. Those predictions have very strong economic basis. Germany is the most populous European country and the world's fifth-largest economic power. The global economic crisis of recent years didn't hit them as hard as other countries. Their economy has been already fully recovered and this year economic growth is predicted to amount 1.9%. Another reason making Scotland, as well as other parts of the United Kingdom, more attractive to German visitors is weakening currency - the exchange rate of the British pound against the Euro has dropped about 25% in the last six years.

Annual leave period available for German employees is longer than in most other countries. The average annual leave entitlement in Germany is 30 days plus up to 10 days of bank holidays. Holidays trips are very important for Germans and they rarely want to give it up, even if the economic conditions are unfavourable.

The most popular directions among Germans are Mediterranean countries - France, Italy and Spain - and nearby Austria. The United Kingdom is the eighth popular destination.


Germans start planning and booking their holidays earlier than people in other countries and that confirms the widely believed stereotype about Germans as a highly organized nation. Over the half of Germans starts to plan and book their vacation at least 3 months earlier.

Most Germans book their trips by retail agencies, but in the recent years more and more people book accommodation and transport for holiday directly on the providers' websites.
Germans don't trust so much in the information from internet websites as people from other countries. When planning and arranging holiday trips, they prefer to get information from travel guides (70%) and local tourist board (xx%).
